Reading Responses

My Website is a Shifting House — Laurel Schwulst

Schwulst compares a website to a house that can grow, change, and shift over time. I like this idea because it makes the web feel more personal and creative instead of something that has to be perfectly finished. A house can have many rooms, different styles, and things added over time, and a website can work the same way. It does not have to be static or polished all at once. I also like the idea that a website can reflect the person who made it, like a space someone lives in. It made me think about my own website as something that can keep evolving as I add projects and ideas.

A Rant on Technology — Ursula K. Le Guin

Le Guin argues that people often talk about technology as if it is something separate from humans, when really it is just something people create and use. She points out that technology can include many different tools and systems, not just modern machines or computers. I thought it was interesting how she challenges the way people think about the word technology. It made me realize that even simple tools or creative processes could be considered technology. This reading also made me think about websites differently, because coding and building websites by hand is also a kind of technology that people actively shape.

A Handmade Web — J.R. Carpenter

Carpenter talks about the idea of the web being something that can be made by hand, instead of relying only on templates or automated tools. The reading emphasizes the importance of learning how websites actually work and experimenting with code. I liked the idea that handmade websites can feel more personal and unique because they reflect the choices of the person creating them. It also connects to the idea that the web does not have to look the same everywhere. Building websites with simple HTML and CSS can be a creative process, almost like crafting something manually.

Against an Increasingly User-Hostile Web — Parimal Satyal

This reading talks about how many modern websites are becoming frustrating to use because they prioritize profit, ads, and data collection over the user experience. Satyal points out how things like pop ups, tracking, autoplay media, and confusing interfaces make the web feel hostile rather than helpful. I found this interesting because it made me realize how often we accept these design choices as normal even though they make the experience worse. It also connects to the idea of building more intentional and human centered websites. Creating simple websites by hand can push back against these patterns and focus more on clarity, creativity, and the actual needs of the user.

A Friend is Writing — Callum Copley

In this reading, Copley describes the experience of watching someone write and thinking about writing as a shared or social act rather than something completely private. The idea that writing can feel like communication with a friend stood out to me. It made the act of writing feel more personal and conversational instead of something formal or distant. I also liked how the text reflects on the space between the writer and the reader, and how writing can create a connection even when people are not physically together. It made me think about how the internet and websites can also work in this way, as spaces where people share thoughts and experiences with others.

The Internet’s Back-to-the-Land Movement — Becca Abbe

This reading talks about a movement where people are returning to more independent and self made spaces on the internet instead of relying on large platforms and social media. The comparison to the back to the land movement is interesting because it frames the web as something people can cultivate themselves, similar to growing their own food or living more sustainably. I liked the idea that building personal websites can be a way of reclaiming creativity and control online. It also connects to the idea of handmade websites, where people learn the basics of coding and create spaces that feel more personal and less commercial.
